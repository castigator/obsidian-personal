Timeline of common changes after quitting

- 20 minutes: heart rate and blood pressure begin to drop toward normal.
- 8–12 hours: carbon monoxide levels in blood fall to normal, improving oxygen delivery.
- 48 hours: nicotine largely cleared; nerve endings begin to regrow, taste and smell improve.
- 2 weeks to 3 months: circulation improves, lung function (measured by FEV1) increases; walking and exercise tolerance get easier.
- 3 to 9 months: cough, wheeze and shortness of breath typically decrease as cilia (the tiny hair-like structures that clear mucus) regenerate and mucus clearance improves. Respiratory infections become less frequent.
- 1 year: risk of coronary heart disease about half that of a continuing smoker.
- 5 years: stroke risk falls to near that of a non-smoker for many people; risk of some cancers (e.g., mouth, throat, esophagus, bladder) is significantly lower.
- 10 years: lung cancer risk is about half that of a continuing smoker (varies by exposure and individual factors); risk of other cancers continues to decline.
- 15+ years: cardiovascular risk can approach that of a never-smoker for many individuals.
