## Universal Credit — Not Applicable

Heather would **not qualify** for Universal Credit post-settlement:
- With equity of £57k+ in savings, she exceeds the £16,000 capital limit
- Even without savings, at £1,471/mo earnings with no children, the 55% taper wipes out the £400.14 standard allowance entirely
- Housing element would only apply if savings were under £16,000

UC is not a factor in Heather's post-settlement finances unless she spends down her equity below £16k (which would take years).

## Key Arguments for Clean Break

1. **Heather is employed full-time** with her own income — the court expects her to maximise earning capacity
2. **No dependent children at home** — housing needs are for one adult only
3. **At low-end costs she is self-sufficient** — no shortfall at all if she rents modestly
4. **At mid-range costs, her equity bridges the gap for 18–51 years** (depending on sale scenario) — taking her well past state pension age
5. **She likely has two DB pensions** — Prudential Staff Pension Scheme plus an Essex LGPS pension from her current school role (LGPS schemes have ~20% employer contributions and can have substantial CETVs). The court should consider this strong retirement provision
6. **Fixed-term vs open-ended** — courts now favour time-limited maintenance if any; a lump sum achieves the same outcome more cleanly
7. **She is the same age as Rupert** — both face the same retirement timeline

## Risks to This Argument

- The income disparity is significant (4:1 ratio) — a court may still award short-term maintenance
- 27-year marriage gives weight to "established living standards"
- If Heather's DB pension CETV is very low, the court may factor retirement needs into maintenance
- A solicitor may argue Heather shouldn't have to run down her equity to meet living costs
