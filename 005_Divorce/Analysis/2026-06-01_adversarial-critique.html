Adversarial Critique — The Hardest Case Against You

Prepared 1 June 2026 | A deliberate devil's-advocate exercise, written in the voice of a cynical opposing solicitor

This document was commissioned by Rupert as a red-team / stress-test of his own position ahead of solicitor consultations. It is written deliberately from the most hostile viewpoint — either as Heather's solicitor would argue, or as worst-case counsel for Rupert. It is not a balanced assessment of likely outcomes, and it is not legal advice. Its purpose is to expose the weakest points in the strategy before someone who is paid to do so finds them first.
Pour me a coffee and pretend I'm sitting across the table — either as Heather's solicitor, or as the brutal one you'd want on your side telling you what you don't want to hear. Either way, I'm going to take this case apart, and find the holes before someone who's paid to does.

1. The headline objective is internally contradictory

Three things are wanted at once: 50/50, a clean break, and no maintenance. In a case like this you may not get to have all three. Possibly not even two.

Here's the vice. "50/50" is the sharing principle — the starting point, not the finish line. It gets overridden by need. So talk about need. Heather is ~59. She nets £1,471 a month. She is a school office manager — her income is not going to leap. At 59, on that income, her mortgage-raising capacity is close to zero; no lender is giving her a meaningful 15-year mortgage. So ask the only question the court actually cares about: can she rehouse and live on her half? If the answer is no — and on these numbers it's probably no — then she does not get half. She gets more. Needs trump sharing in every ordinary-money case, and this is an ordinary-money case dressed up with a complicated house.

And here's where the logic turns. If the capital can't meet her needs, the court has exactly two levers: give her more capital (eat into your share and your pensions) or order maintenance. Refusing both at once is not an available option. Pick your poison — but stop pretending the menu doesn't exist.

2. The clean break is the weakest brick in the wall

"Courts favour fixed-term orders" — yes, and a fixed term is not a clean break. "Fixed-term maintenance" and "clean break" have quietly become the same sentence in these notes. They are opposites. For a wife of 59, married 27 years, four-to-one income gap, a term order running to State Pension age — or frankly joint lives — is not some outlandish ask from a greedy lawyer. It is the textbook outcome these facts invite.

The counter — "she's employed, therefore self-sufficient" — is worth nothing. Being employed at ~£17.6k gross does not make you self-sufficient at the standard of a 27-year marriage. That argument is dismantled in two lines.

And the affordability model — the one that "bridges her shortfall by spending down equity for 20+ years" — hear how it sounds read aloud in a hearing:

"My client's husband, who keeps his £6,000 a month and his pensions, proposes that his wife of 27 years slowly eat her only capital cushion to subsidise his clean break."

The whole point of maintenance is to stop the weaker party consuming capital to survive. That spreadsheet proves the exact thing the court is trying to prevent. Handed to Heather's solicitor, it drafts their cross-examination for them.

3. The main event is being treated as a footnote: pensions + age

Pensions are filed under "the main complication, address via offsetting." Wrong filing. Both parties are about 60. This is not a couple in their forties who'll recover earning capacity. Retirement is the live issue, now — so the pension is not a complication, it may be the case.

Tot it up honestly. There is ~£206k traced and two schemes that can't even be found — Speedware (7 years) and Conchango (6 years). That's thirteen years of pension accrual during prime earning life, unaccounted for. An opponent doesn't treat those as "untraced." They treat them as hidden until proven otherwise, and make the incomplete disclosure your problem, not theirs. Heather's are smaller and messier, but if she's LGPS through the school, that's gold-plated defined benefit and it will be valued properly.

Net of all that, the probable shape is a large pension-sharing order against you to equalise retirement income — and offsetting won't rescue it, because there isn't enough house equity to offset a six-figure pension gap. You cannot buy your way out of the pension claim with a house that's half-eaten by a mortgage and possibly subsiding.

And don't reach for "but Midel and Speedware pre-date the marriage." In a needs case the matrimonial / non-matrimonial line gets painted over. Ring-fencing pre-1998 accrual is an argument for a rich couple. It isn't available here.

4. The document intended for Heather may be the worst move on the board

A day has gone into polishing a "neutral" cost leaflet to put in front of her. Here's what lands on the opposing desk when she brings it in.

A litigant in person — the financially stronger one — handing the weaker, represented party a professionally-styled document whose entire thrust is "don't lawyer up, mediation is best, cooperation is cheap." That gets read to her in about thirty seconds:

"He is the one with the £6,000 salary and the £200,000 of pension. He has done months of homework. You have not. And he's brought you a pamphlet telling you that getting proper advice is a waste of money. Why do you suppose the man holding all the cards wants you across a table with a mediator who is forbidden from telling you what you're entitled to?"

The "every pound on solicitors comes out of the pot" line turns straight around:

"The £3,000 of advice that wins you an extra £60,000 of pension share is the best-value money you will ever spend. Don't let him talk you out of it with a leaflet."

With genuine effort and good production values, the leaflet becomes the single most persuasive argument for why Heather should instruct a solicitor and dig in. The more imbalanced and information-poor she is, the more a switched-on solicitor will say mediation is unsafe for her — and the document advertises that imbalance in glossy A4.

There's a sharper edge, too. Pushed too far, a litigant in person steering a represented party away from independent advice looks like exactly that — and it's not a flattering look if it ever surfaces. At best it's clumsy. At worst it hardens her and tells her solicitor you're anxious to do this on the cheap.

5. The urgency is the weakness — and it keeps being advertised

Every document flashes the same neon sign: £4,000 a month is bleeding out. That's supposed to be the engine for a quick settlement. From the other side of the table it's the opposite — it's leverage for Heather to go slow.

Reframe it the hostile way. You moved out. You set up home with a new partner. You chose to keep paying 80% — nobody made you. The dual-housing pain is self-inflicted and one-directional: it hurts you, not her. She is sitting in the house. Time is unambiguously on her side. So her optimal play is patience — let the meter run while disclosure takes its time and your appetite for a deal grows by the month.

And the relief being banked on isn't guaranteed, because you cannot simply force a sale. An order for sale is not a foregone conclusion when the weaker party is in occupation and needs a home; expect Mesher arguments and delay. The exit isn't in your gift.

6. The optics are not your friend

Be blunt about the human picture, because judges are human. On one side: the higher earner who left, comfortably cohabiting with a new partner who shares his bills, needs reduced, pension intact. On the other: the wife left behind on £1,471 a month, in a cracking, subsiding house, with the four cats. No need to argue sympathy — just describe the room.

And the new partner cuts against you twice. Her income isn't divisible — true, but that's not the point. The point is that cohabitation reduces your needs, and reduced needs for you means more available for Heather. Your household is subsidised; hers isn't.

7. The well-prepared-litigant trap

The most cynical observation, and the one worth sitting with. An enormous amount of work has gone in — briefing packs, spreadsheets, cost models, solicitor dossiers. But look at what it all points at: justifying a conclusion decided at the start. 50/50, clean break, no maintenance. Every document assembles evidence for the desired answer. That's not analysis; it's advocacy for your own comfort. The well-prepared litigant's disease is confirmation bias with footnotes.

The most valuable hour bought at a consultation is the one where the solicitor says the unwelcome thing — that the target outcome is probably not what a court would order — and that hour is not spent arguing back. Walk out having "won" the meeting and it was wasted.

What would actually change the assessment

Two things would genuinely change a hostile assessment — so these are what to actually chase:

  1. Heather's pensions, valued in full. If her LGPS / Prudential DB pots are larger than anyone's assuming, the pension gap narrows and the clean-break case gets real legs. Right now this is unknown — and not knowing is costing the argument.
  2. A defensible figure for what Heather actually needs to rehouse locally. If — and only if — her share of equity genuinely buys or substantially funds a modest home nearby, the clean break stops being fantasy. That housing-need number is the whole ballgame. Build that model, not the one where she spends down her savings.

Everything else — the leaflet, the £4k/month lament, the 50/50 mantra — is either neutral or working against you.

So: convince me. Why should Heather take less than her needs require, give up a maintenance claim the law hands her on a plate, and accept a pension settlement before you've even found two of your own schemes? I'm listening — but I'm not nodding.
