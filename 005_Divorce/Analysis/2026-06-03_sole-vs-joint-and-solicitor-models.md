# Sole vs Joint Divorce Application — and How Many Solicitors

**Date:** 3 June 2026
**Context:** Working through the [[Divorce]] process-flow diagram (`Analysis/2026-06-02_divorce-process-flow-validated.md`), Rupert questioned whether the early divorce boxes (File application → Application issued → Acknowledge service) are right. That surfaced a decision he hasn't made: **sole vs joint application** — and a follow-on question about **how many solicitors** are involved.

> **Not legal advice.** This is the mechanics of the process so Rupert can make an informed choice. Confirm specifics with Alan Caldwell (Atkins Dellow).

---

## The question asked

1. Sole vs joint application — what's the actual difference?
2. If we apply **jointly**, do we each have to have our own solicitor?
3. If we **share** a solicitor, can one solicitor do a joint application?

---

## 1. Sole vs Joint — what each is

- **Sole application** — Rupert applies as the "applicant"; Heather is the "respondent." The court serves her the application; she returns an **Acknowledgement of Service** (~14 days) confirming receipt. From then on **Rupert drives every step** — applies for the Conditional Order, then the Final Order. Heather is largely a passenger.
- **Joint application** — both apply together as "Applicant 1" and "Applicant 2." **No respondent, no service, no acknowledgement of service.** At each stage **both** are expected to act (jointly apply for the Conditional Order, jointly for the Final Order).

---

## 2. What does NOT change with sole vs joint (the key myth-buster)

The choice is about **control and tone, not outcome.**

- **No blame either way** — it's no-fault. A sole application is **not** adversarial; Heather can't be blamed and can't "lose."
- **Zero effect on money** — the divorce doesn't decide finances at all; that's the separate consent order. Sole/joint has **no bearing** on the 50/50, pensions, or the fairness test.
- **Same cost** — one **£612** court fee either way (not doubled for joint).
- **Same timeline** — 20 weeks from issue, then 6 weeks + 1 day. Identical.
- **Same end result** — marriage ends the same way.

---

## 3. Where they actually differ

| | **Sole** (Rupert applies) | **Joint** |
|---|---|---|
| Who controls the pace | **Rupert — entirely** | Needs **both** to act at each stage |
| Reliance on Heather | She does one thing once (return AoS) | She must participate at **every** milestone |
| If cooperation stalls | Doesn't affect Rupert — he drives on | Can stall the process (convertible to sole later) |
| Tone / optics | Some perceive being "served" as adversarial (it isn't) | Feels collaborative; reinforces the amicable route |

**Convertibility:** a joint application can later be continued by **one** applicant as a sole applicant if the other won't proceed (at the Conditional/Final Order stages) — so joint isn't a permanent trap if Heather disengages, but it can introduce delay.

---

## 4. How this lands on Rupert's situation

Two competing pressures:

- **Cost of delay is brutal** — ~£4k/month in dual housing costs. Argues for **sole**: Rupert keeps his hand on the throttle, never waiting for Heather to log in and do her bit at each stage.
- **Whole strategy is cooperative** (mediation → fixed-fee consent order). Argues for **joint**: sets the collaborative tone.

**Assessment:** these are less opposed than they look. A **sole application by Rupert is still entirely non-adversarial** under no-fault — Heather isn't blamed and loses nothing — but it keeps **Rupert controlling the timeline**, which given the £4k/month bleed is worth a lot. She only has to return one form (the AoS). The cooperative spirit lives in **mediation and the consent order**, which is where it actually matters — not in whose name is on the divorce application.

**The one caveat** is perception: if being the sole applicant would *feel* aggressive to Heather and sour the cooperation needed for the finances, that perception cost might outweigh the control benefit. That's a read on Heather only Rupert can make.

## 5. How many solicitors? (Independent of sole vs joint)

**Number of solicitors is completely independent of sole-vs-joint.** A joint application can run with:

- **Zero** solicitors (both DIY / litigants in person),
- **One** solicitor (acting for one party, or — see below — for both), or
- **Two** solicitors (one each).

**Joint does NOT imply "one each."** It just means both are named applicants.

### Can one solicitor act for both?

- **For the divorce itself** — **yes, easily.** A joint no-fault application is non-contentious paperwork; one solicitor can act for both.
- **For the finances / consent order** — **yes, but only under a safeguarded model:** **Resolution Together** ("one couple, one lawyer"), developed by Resolution (the national family-law body) and **permitted by the SRA**, provided conflicts are managed.

### How the "one couple, one lawyer" model works (the safeguards)

- The solicitor must be **specially trained** in the model.
- Before any joint work, the solicitor **meets each spouse separately** to confirm: both want to engage openly, there's **no abuse/coercion/significant power imbalance**, and no conflict that would prevent joint advice.
- The solicitor advises the couple **together, impartially**, aiming at a **fair** agreement.
- **If a genuine conflict or deadlock arises, the solicitor must withdraw from advising both** — each spouse then takes separate legal advice.
- The agreement is converted into a **consent order** and submitted to court for approval, exactly as in any negotiated settlement.

**This is almost certainly what Alan Caldwell meant** when he said (2 Jun consultation) that Heather could "agree the consent order without her own independent advice" / come through the same single-solicitor flow. The known **risk** of the unrepresented spouse not having independent advice: she could later claim she didn't understand what she agreed to — though the court's **fairness check** (via Form D81) and the model's safeguards mitigate this.

## 6. Implications for the process-flow diagram

- The **"Acknowledgement of service" box is sole-application-only.** On a joint application it **disappears entirely**.
- Even on a sole application, AoS is **mis-positioned** in the current diagram: it sits *inside* the 20-week wait (concurrent, early), **not** as a step that delays the start of the 20 weeks. The **20-week clock starts from the issue date**, regardless of when AoS is returned. (See the separate process-flow validation note.)
