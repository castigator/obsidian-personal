# Other Income & Inheritance — Impact on Divorce Settlement

**Date:** 18 April 2026
**Context:** Rupert asked whether Mandy Zimmer's income or potential parental inheritances could be factored into the divorce settlement.

**Disclaimer: This is not legal advice. These are general principles under England & Wales family law — a solicitor should confirm how they apply to your specific case.**

---

## Question 1: Does Mandy's income count?

**Short answer: No, not directly — but it could be indirectly relevant.**

### The law

A new partner's income is **not** a matrimonial asset and cannot be divided as part of the settlement. The court is splitting assets and income accumulated during the marriage between the two spouses.

However, under **Section 25 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973**, the court must consider "all the circumstances of the case." This means:

- If Rupert is cohabiting with Mandy and she contributes to household costs, the court may consider that Rupert's **needs** are reduced (because he's sharing rent, bills, etc.)
- Conversely, the court might reason that Rupert has more disposable income because Mandy shares his living costs — making him better able to pay maintenance or accept a smaller share of assets
- Mandy's income **cannot be ring-fenced or claimed** by Heather, but it forms part of the financial landscape the court considers

### Practical impact

- If Heather's solicitor raises spousal maintenance, they may argue that Rupert's outgoings are lower because Mandy shares costs — therefore he can afford to pay maintenance
- The counter-argument: Mandy is not a party to the marriage and has no obligation to subsidise Heather. The court should assess Rupert's needs based on his own income and reasonable outgoings
- Case law (particularly **Grey v Grey [2009]**) confirms that a new partner's resources should not be treated as available to meet the other spouse's claims, but that the benefit of shared living costs can be acknowledged

### What this means for Rupert

- Mandy's income won't be divided or counted as Rupert's
- But the fact Rupert shares housing costs with Mandy could slightly weaken a "I can't afford maintenance" argument
- This is **not a major risk** — it's a factor, not a determinant

## Question 2: Do parental inheritances count?

**Short answer: Almost certainly not, in the current circumstances.**

### The people involved

| Person | Relationship | Situation |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Gordon | Heather's father | Alzheimer's, unlikely to live much longer |
| Heather's mother + David (stepfather) | Heather's parents | Alive |
| Stuart Thomas | Rupert's father | Alive; personal loan of £3,000 to Rupert |

### The law

**Future/potential inheritances are NOT matrimonial assets.** The court deals with assets that exist at the time of the settlement, not speculative future windfalls.

Key principles:

1. **No one has a right to inherit.** Parents can change their wills, spend their money, or leave it to charity. An expectation of inheritance is not an asset.

2. **Section 25 factors** require the court to consider "the financial resources which each of the parties to the marriage has or is likely to have in the foreseeable future." The word "likely" has been interpreted narrowly — a vague expectation of inheritance doesn't qualify.

3. **Case law — P v P [2004]**: The court held that a potential inheritance should only be considered where it is "virtually certain" (e.g., an irrevocable trust already established). An elderly parent who might leave money does not meet this threshold.

4. **Case law — MT v MT [1992]**: Future inheritance was described as a "speculative resource" and excluded from the financial order.

5. **Received inheritances during the marriage** can be treated as matrimonial property and divided — but **unreceived, future inheritances** cannot.

### Gordon (Heather's father, Alzheimer's)

Even though Gordon may pass away relatively soon:
- Until he dies, there is no inheritance — it doesn't exist yet
- His estate could be depleted by care home fees (Alzheimer's care is extremely expensive and can consume an entire estate)
- His will could leave everything to Heather's mother, a charity, or anyone else
- The court would not factor in a speculative windfall to Heather

**If Gordon dies and Heather inherits before the financial order is finalised**, that received inheritance could theoretically be considered. This is a timing question — another reason to progress the consent order promptly.

### Heather's mother and David

Even more remote. Both alive, no indication of imminent death. Pure speculation — the court will not consider this.

### Stuart (Rupert's father)

Same principle. Stuart is alive, the £3,000 loan is a personal debt (not matrimonial), and any future inheritance is speculative. It won't factor in.

## Summary

| Source | Counts in settlement? | Risk level |
|--------|----------------------|------------|
| Mandy's income | **No** (not divisible), but shared living costs could be noted | Low |
| Gordon's inheritance (Heather) | **No** — speculative until received | Very low |
| Heather's mother/David inheritance | **No** — even more remote | Negligible |
| Stuart's inheritance (Rupert) | **No** — speculative | Negligible |

### Recommended actions

1. **Don't raise inheritance proactively** — there's no benefit to flagging it. If Heather's solicitor raises Gordon's situation, the response is straightforward: it's speculative and not an asset.
2. **Progress the consent order before Gordon passes** — if Heather inherits before the financial order is sealed, it becomes a live asset. Timing matters.
3. **On Mandy's income** — if asked, be transparent about shared living costs but make clear Mandy's income is hers, not Rupert's. A solicitor can handle this.
4. **Get a solicitor** — these questions are exactly why you need one. A Resolution-accredited family lawyer will know how your local court typically handles these factors.
