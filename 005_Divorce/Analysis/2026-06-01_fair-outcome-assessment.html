Fair Outcome Assessment — The Balanced View

Prepared 1 June 2026 | The counterweight to the adversarial critique — what a fair and reasonable settlement realistically looks like

This is the balanced counterpart to the adversarial critique of the same date. That document was deliberately one-sided — its job was to find every weakness. This one is the honest middle. It is an estimate of a realistic, fair outcome, not legal advice, and it rests on one genuine unknown — the value of Heather's pensions — which moves everything. Figures are rough planning illustrations, not predictions.

The one fact that reframes everything

Roughly two-thirds of the combined wealth is pension, not house. Net house equity is perhaps £150,000–£200,000 to split; combined pensions are probably £300,000+. And both parties are about 60. Those two facts together change the picture — and they cut for Rupert as well as against:

  • The income gap that powers Heather's maintenance claim has a natural expiry — State Pension age, ~6–7 years away. After that, if pensions are equalised, retirement incomes are broadly similar. The gap is not a lifelong problem; it is a bridge of a few years.
  • That makes a clean break at or near retirement genuinely realistic — the opposite of the "joint lives forever" spectre. A long open-ended maintenance order is much less likely because retirement is close and pensions can do the equalising.

What a fair outcome looks like, by component

1. The house — tilted to Heather, but not by much in aggregate

She gets the larger share of the realisable capital, probably 60–65%, for one unglamorous reason: at 59 on ~£17.6k gross she cannot raise a meaningful mortgage, and she needs a roof. Rupert can borrow, already rents, and shares a household with his partner. So she needs more of the liquid money. That is a borrowing-capacity point, not a moral one. Ballpark: her ~£100,000–£130,000, Rupert ~£55,000–£75,000 from the house.

2. Pensions — shared roughly equally, the offset to the house tilt

Long marriage, pensions as the dominant asset: the default is equal sharing of total pension wealth via a pension sharing order moving value from Rupert to Heather. Rupert keeps proportionally more pension to offset her taking more house — that is how the aggregate stays fair. The size of the transfer depends entirely on her CETVs, which are not yet known. It could be a modest top-up; it could be substantial.

3. Maintenance — probably a short bridge, or bought out

Two realistic versions:

  • A fixed-term order running to roughly retirement, modest, possibly stepping down — bridging her income until pensions crystallise. Then it ends.
  • A capitalised clean break — Rupert pays a lump sum in lieu (from his equity / pension share) and her maintenance claim is dismissed. This is how the clean break is actually achieved. A clean break is not refused into existence; it is paid for.

Whether full capitalisation is affordable depends on whether there is enough capital. If the pot is thin, the outcome lands on the short term order instead.

4. Debts

Joint debts (~£25,000) come off the top or are shared; Ann's £8,800 informal loans are contestable and may be discounted as soft family loans; Rupert's personal ~£7,000 stays his. The banks are not bound by any of this — decoupling means actually paying down or transferring balances, not simply allocating them in the order.

The honest bottom line

In aggregate, a fair outcome is close to 50/50 of total wealth — so the core instinct is not wrong. Where reality departs from the stated goal is the structure:

  • Not "half of each asset and a clean walk-away," but more house to her, more pension to him, netting out near even;
  • plus a bridging element — either a few years of term maintenance or a capital sum that buys the clean break;
  • with the clean break achievable at or around retirement, which is genuinely the strongest card.

So the 50/50 aggregate survives. What does not survive is "no maintenance, no pension share, simple half-each" — that specific package is the optimistic part.

What decides where in the range it lands

Two numbers, both currently blank, and both for the solicitor meeting:

  1. Heather's pension CETVs (Prudential DB + likely Essex LGPS + the untraced schemes) — and Rupert's two missing schemes (Speedware, Conchango). The closer her pensions are to his, the smaller the share she needs and the easier the clean break.
  2. A real figure for Heather's local housing need — what actually rehouses her. If her share of equity genuinely does it, the clean break is clean. If it does not, that gap becomes either more capital to her or a maintenance tail.

Get those two and the fog turns into an actual settlement range.
