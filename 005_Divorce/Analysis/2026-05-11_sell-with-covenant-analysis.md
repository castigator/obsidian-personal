## Selling with a Covenant — How It Could Work

### The concept

Sell the property at a discount reflecting the ongoing subsidence works, but with the benefit of:
- Confirmed insurer liability (the hard part is done)
- An active claim that will fund repairs
- A known timeline and professional loss adjusters managing the process

### What a buyer's solicitor would need to see

1. **Confirmation of liability acceptance** — we have this (11 May 2026 GHG letter)
2. **The insurance policy details** — PO Home Insurance H0000011333, renewed May 2026
3. **Whether the claim can transfer** — key question. Home insurance claims typically attach to the policyholder, not the property. On sale:
   - The current policy would cancel
   - The buyer would need their own insurance (subsidence-affected properties are hard to insure)
   - The claim might need to be settled/assigned before completion
4. **Repair cost estimate** — not available until Dec 2026
5. **Certificate of Structural Adequacy** — only issued after repairs are complete and monitoring confirms stabilisation

### Realistic scenarios

**Scenario A: Sell after tree removal + monitoring confirms stabilisation (est. Jan 2027)**
- Buyer knows the claim is active, repairs funded, trees already removed
- Discount likely smaller — main uncertainty is just repair completion
- Price: perhaps £480k–£510k (between current £450k subsidence-affected and £550k clean)

**Scenario B: Sell now (May–Jul 2026) with claim in progress**
- Trees not yet removed, repairs not scoped, monitoring ongoing
- Much larger discount — buyer takes more risk
- Policy transferability is the key blocker
- Price: likely still around £450k or less — not much better than selling with unresolved subsidence

**Scenario C: Wait for repairs to complete (est. Apr–May 2027)**
- Full value achievable (~£550k)
- But another 12 months of dual housing costs (~£48k)
- Net benefit vs. selling now: £100k uplift minus £48k carrying cost = ~£52k benefit
- Net benefit vs. Scenario A: ~£40k–£70k uplift minus ~£16k additional carrying cost

## Recommendation

The liability acceptance is the critical unlock, but **selling with a covenant before repairs are scoped (Dec 2026) is likely to achieve a similar price to selling with unresolved subsidence** — because the buyer can't quantify what they're inheriting.

The realistic early-sale window is probably **after monitoring confirms stabilisation and repair costs are known (Jan 2027)**. At that point you'd have: liability accepted, trees removed, repairs costed and funded. A buyer can price the remaining risk accurately.

**Immediate action:** Ask a solicitor whether PO claim transferability is feasible. That determines whether this route is even possible.
