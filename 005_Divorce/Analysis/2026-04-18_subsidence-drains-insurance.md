## Key Findings

### 1. Subsidence cause: tree roots, NOT drains

GHG's April 2026 letter is definitive:

> "The drainage survey completed as part of our investigations identified several defects with the drains, but these are not thought to be implicated in the current subsidence movement. The ground was found to be dry and very stiff, which rules them out as a potential influence on the soils."

The confirmed cause is **root-induced clay shrinkage**:
- Maple tree (Acer spp.) — 4.5m from extension, roots confirmed at foundation level (TP2/BH2: roots to 5mm diameter at 0.6m, abundant starch)
- Willow tree (Salix spp.) — 12.5m from front of property, roots confirmed at foundation level (TP1/BH1: roots at 1.6m depth, moderate starch)
- Clay soils classified CI (intermediate plasticity), liquid limits 44-47%, very stiff (shear vane >150 kPa throughout)
- Foundations are shallow: TP1 at 1.45m (250mm concrete), TP2 at 0.6m (300mm concrete)

### 2. Drain damage is separate — caused by tree root ingress

The CCTV survey found extensive drain damage across most runs:

- **3 runs graded Urgent (red):** Runs 1, 6, 8 — root masses causing 100% blockage, root ingress
- **5 runs graded Slight (yellow):** Runs 2, 3, 4, 5, RWG/2 — displacements, cracks, root penetration
- **2 runs OK (green):** Runs 7, RWG/1 (RWG/1 is off boundary/neighbour's)

GHG explicitly states the drain damage is "the result of physical tree root damage / ingress and is remote from the area of subsidence movement."

### 3. Drain repair cost: £9,360.63 + VAT

The DW Solutions Recommendation Sheet itemises all repairs (root cutting, liners, patches, HPWJ, gully work) totalling £9,360.63 + VAT.

### 4. GHG recommends a separate Post Office claim

> "We would advise you to contact your Insurers to submit a separate drainage claim, which would normally fall for consideration under accidental damage to underground services cover."

This is a standard recommendation — most buildings insurance policies include accidental damage to underground services as a named peril.

## Analysis: HomeServe vs Post Office for drain claim

| Factor | HomeServe (443906608) | Post Office (H0000011333) |
|--------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Claim limit | £4,000 per claim | Buildings sum insured (typically rebuild cost) |
| Repair cost | £9,360.63 | £9,360.63 |
| Excess | £0 | £1,000 (subsidence excess — drainage excess may differ) |
| Net recovery | **£4,000 max** | **~£8,360** |
| Shortfall | ~£5,360 uninsured | £1,000 excess only |
| Cover type | Plumbing & drainage maintenance | Accidental damage to underground services |
| Suitability | Designed for blockages/leaks, not structural rehab | Designed for this type of damage |
| Risk | Could complicate the subsidence claim if PO argues drains dealt with elsewhere | Clean — GHG recommends this route |

**Conclusion: Post Office is clearly the right route.** HomeServe can't cover it (£4k limit vs £9.3k bill) and using HomeServe could create complications with the subsidence claim.

### 5. Why Heather not claiming matters

- £8,360 of insured loss sitting unclaimed
- The drains need fixing regardless — they'll show up on any buyer's survey
- The tree roots causing drain damage are the same trees causing subsidence — once removed (target Jul 2026), the drain damage won't worsen, but existing damage still needs repair
- GHG has already flagged this to the insurer (CC'd on April letter) — the groundwork is done
