Divorce Costs — What to Expect

What a consent order typically costs each party, and what happens if agreement cannot be reached

How the Process Works

A consent order is the legal document that settles finances on divorce — property, pensions, debts, and maintenance. It's drafted by solicitors, agreed by both parties, and approved by a judge. Each party pays their own solicitor separately.

The cost depends almost entirely on how quickly agreement can be reached. The stages below are the same regardless of which solicitor is instructed — the only variable is the hourly rate and how many hours each stage takes.

Stages and Typical Hours

1. Initial consultation & advice (1–2 hours)

  • Review the financial position
  • Advise on likely outcomes and strategy
  • Discuss whether a pension expert (PODE) is needed

2. Financial disclosure / Form E (3–6 hours)

  • Each party completes Form E (the standard financial disclosure form)
  • Solicitor reviews, advises, chases missing information
  • Reviewing the other party's Form E
  • Querying valuations, pension values, debt documentation

3. Negotiation and correspondence (5–25+ hours)

This is the biggest cost driver by far. It depends entirely on how cooperative both sides are.

  • Solicitor-to-solicitor letters, emails, and calls
  • Advising on offers and counter-offers
  • Pension sharing vs offsetting analysis
  • Property sale timing, debt allocation, maintenance arguments
Level of agreementTypical hours
Both sides cooperate — light negotiation5–8 hours
Some disagreement — moderate negotiation8–15 hours
Significant disagreement — difficult negotiation15–25+ hours

4. Pension work (2–5 hours)

  • Obtaining formal CETVs (Cash Equivalent Transfer Values) for all pensions
  • If a PODE (Pension on Divorce Expert) is instructed: £1,500–£5,000 for the expert's fee (usually shared between parties)
  • Solicitor time reviewing the expert's report and drafting pension sharing annex

5. Drafting the consent order (2–4 hours)

  • Drafting the consent order itself
  • Preparing the Statement of Information (Form D81)
  • Pension sharing annex if applicable
  • Both parties review and sign

6. Court submission (1–2 hours)

  • Filing with the court (£60 court fee)
  • Responding to any queries from the judge

Each party pays their own solicitor's fees. However, the more both parties spend on legal costs before settlement, the less net wealth remains between them. High combined legal bills can significantly reduce the eventual settlement value for both sides.

What Happens If Agreement Cannot Be Reached

If a consent order cannot be negotiated, the next step is Financial Remedy proceedings — a formal court process. The costs escalate dramatically:

StageAdditional cost per person
Form A to First Appointment+£5,000–£10,000
First Appointment to FDR hearing+£10,000–£15,000
FDR to Final Hearing+£10,000–£20,000
Total contested (per person)£25,000–£50,000+

Combined: £50,000–£100,000+ in legal fees alone. This would consume a very large portion of the equity in a typical property.

Approaches Compared — Why Some Cost More Than Others

The hourly rates for mediators, collaborative lawyers, and traditional solicitors are broadly similar (£150–£400/hr depending on experience and location). The difference in total cost is driven by the number of hours each approach typically requires, not the rate per hour.

1. Mediation + consent order

Typical combined cost: £3,000–£8,000

How it works: Both parties sit in the same room with an independent mediator. Decisions are made directly, face to face. Once agreement is reached, each party gets brief independent legal advice, and a solicitor drafts the consent order for court approval.

Why it's cheaper:

  • Both parties are present — issues get resolved in real time rather than through weeks of letters
  • A typical case takes 3–6 mediation sessions (roughly 6–12 hours of mediator time, shared)
  • There is only one professional in the room, not two solicitors each billing separately
  • Solicitor involvement is limited to legal advice and drafting — perhaps 3–5 hours per party
  • Total professional hours across both parties: roughly 15–25 hours

Drawbacks:

  • Both parties must be willing to participate — it cannot be imposed
  • The mediator is neutral and cannot give legal advice to either party
  • Does not work well if there is a significant power imbalance or if one party is not engaging honestly
  • Each party still needs independent legal advice before signing, which is an additional cost
  • If mediation fails, the time and money spent on it are largely wasted — the case reverts to solicitor negotiation or court

2. Collaborative law

Typical combined cost: £8,000–£15,000

How it works: Each party instructs a collaboratively trained solicitor. All four people meet in structured sessions to negotiate directly. Both solicitors sign an agreement that if the process breaks down and the case goes to court, neither solicitor can continue acting — both parties must instruct new solicitors from scratch.

Why it's mid-range:

  • Decisions are still made face to face, which is more efficient than correspondence
  • Both parties have their own legal adviser present in the room — no need for separate "relay" correspondence
  • The "disqualification" clause creates a strong incentive for both sides to reach agreement
  • Typical total: 3–6 four-way meetings plus preparation time — roughly 20–35 hours of solicitor time across both parties

Drawbacks:

  • Two solicitors are billing simultaneously during each meeting, so session costs are higher than mediation
  • If it fails, both parties lose their solicitor and must start again with new representation — a significant financial and emotional penalty
  • Not all solicitors are collaboratively trained, so choice may be limited
  • Both parties must agree to the process

3. Solicitor-negotiated consent order

Typical combined cost: £10,000–£35,000

How it works: Each party instructs their own solicitor. The solicitors communicate by letter and email to negotiate the terms. The parties do not meet or communicate directly on legal matters — everything goes through the solicitors.

Why it costs more:

  • Two solicitors are billing independently — every exchange generates time on both sides
  • Communication is indirect: Party A tells Solicitor A → Solicitor A writes to Solicitor B → Solicitor B advises Party B → Party B responds → the chain reverses. Each round trip can take days and bill hours on both clocks
  • A point that might be resolved in 20 minutes face to face can generate 2–3 hours of correspondence
  • Typical total: 30–70 hours of solicitor time across both parties

Benefits:

  • Each party has dedicated legal advice throughout, not just at the end
  • Better suited to complex financial cases (multiple pensions, business interests, disputed valuations)
  • No requirement for both parties to be in the same room
  • If negotiations fail, the same solicitors can continue into court proceedings

4. Contested court proceedings (Financial Remedy)

Typical combined cost: £50,000–£100,000+

How it works: If agreement cannot be reached by any of the above methods, either party can apply to the court for a Financial Remedy order. A judge decides the outcome after a series of hearings.

Why it costs so much:

  • The process involves multiple court hearings over 12–18 months (First Appointment, FDR hearing, and potentially a Final Hearing)
  • Each hearing requires preparation: witness statements, position statements, court bundles, valuation evidence
  • Barristers are often instructed in addition to solicitors, adding a further layer of cost
  • Disclosure disputes, adjournments, and procedural arguments all generate additional hours
  • Neither party controls the timetable — the court does
  • Typical total: 80–200+ hours of professional time across both parties

Benefits:

  • A judge makes a binding decision — useful if one party is being unreasonable or refusing to engage
  • Full judicial scrutiny of financial disclosure — harder for either party to hide assets
  • The only option if all other approaches have been refused or have failed

The pattern is clear: the more directly both parties communicate, the fewer professional hours are needed, and the lower the cost. Mediation puts both parties in the room with one neutral professional. Solicitor negotiation separates them entirely and doubles the billing. Court proceedings add barristers, hearings, and months of preparation on top.
